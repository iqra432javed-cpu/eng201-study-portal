3.1 Introduction

Every work of fictional prose, regardless of length or genre, is constructed from a common set of formal elements. This chapter examines the six elements identified in the ENG-201 syllabus as essential to the analysis of fictional prose: plot, character, setting, point of view, theme, and style and tone. These elements do not operate independently; rather, they interact continuously, such that a change in point of view alters how characterization is perceived, and a shift in setting can reinforce or undercut theme. Mastery of this chapter is essential preparation for both the close-reading questions in Section II and the comparative long questions that ask students to analyse how these elements function across multiple texts.

Overview — The Six Elements

3.2 Plot Structure

Plot is the structured sequence of events in a narrative, organised by causation rather than mere chronology. Aristotle's distinction is again instructive here: he distinguished mythos (plot, the arrangement of incidents) from the raw material of events, arguing that plot is the "soul" of tragedy because it is through the deliberate arrangement of cause and effect that meaning is generated. A plot is not simply "what happens" but "what happens, and why, in what order, and to what effect."

3.2.1 Freytag's Pyramid

The German novelist and critic Gustav Freytag proposed in Die Technik des Dramas (1863) a five-part model of dramatic structure, subsequently applied widely to prose fiction. The five stages are:

  • Exposition: Introduces setting, characters, and the initial situation before conflict emerges.
  • Rising Action: A series of complications that intensify conflict and build tension.
  • Climax: The turning point of maximum tension, after which the outcome becomes inevitable.
  • Falling Action: The unspooling of consequences that follow the climax.
  • Denouement (Resolution): The final state of equilibrium, whether resolved or tragically unresolved.
Diagram — Freytag's Pyramid

3.2.2 Beyond Freytag: Plot in Non-Linear Fiction

While Freytag's model remains pedagogically useful, students should understand its limits when analysing more complex narrative structures. Moth Smoke, for instance, does not unfold in simple chronological sequence; its trial-frame structure means the reader receives the "climax" (Daru's alleged crime) only gradually, reconstructed through fragmented testimony. Such structures are sometimes described using the Russian Formalist distinction between fabula (the chronological sequence of events as they "really" occurred in the storyworld) and syuzhet (the order in which those events are actually presented to the reader). A sophisticated long-question answer on plot should be able to invoke this distinction when discussing non-linear texts.

🎯 Examiner Tip

When asked to apply Freytag's Pyramid to Lord of the Flies, identify the climax precisely: most examiners accept either Simon's murder (the moral climax) or the final hunt for Ralph (the structural climax). Naming both and explaining the distinction demonstrates advanced understanding.

3.3 Character and Characterization

Characterization is the set of techniques an author uses to construct and reveal character. The distinction between direct (explicit) and indirect (dramatic) characterization, originating in classical rhetoric and refined by modern narratology, remains the most examinable framework in this area.

3.3.1 Methods of Characterization

  • Direct characterization: The narrator explicitly states a character's traits ("Piggy was fat and wheezed").
  • Indirect characterization: Traits are revealed through speech, action, thought, appearance, and the reactions of others — requiring active interpretive work from the reader.

3.3.2 E. M. Forster's Round and Flat Characters

In Aspects of the Novel (1927), E. M. Forster proposed the influential distinction between round characters, who are complex, psychologically multidimensional, and "capable of surprising in a convincing way," and flat characters, who are constructed around a single dominant trait or function and do not develop. Forster did not consider flat characters inferior — they serve important structural and comic functions — but he reserved his highest praise for the novel's capacity to render genuinely round human consciousness.

Applying this framework: Daru in Moth Smoke is a round character whose moral complexity resists easy judgement, while many of the boys in Lord of the Flies beyond the central five (Ralph, Jack, Piggy, Simon, Roger) function as a relatively flat, undifferentiated "chorus" representing the broader social body.

3.3.3 Dynamic and Static Characters

A further distinction concerns change over time: a dynamic character undergoes significant internal transformation across the narrative (Jack's transformation from choirboy to tribal leader), while a static character remains essentially unchanged (Piggy's rationalism is constant from beginning to end, which is itself thematically significant — his failure to adapt contributes to his marginalisation and death).

⭐ Examiner Favourite

"Differentiate between round and flat characters, with reference to a course text" is a recurring short question. Always cite Forster by name and provide one example of each type from a named text.

3.4 Setting

Setting comprises the time, place, and social/cultural environment within which a narrative unfolds. Setting is frequently underestimated by students as a passive "backdrop," but in sophisticated fiction it performs active structural and thematic work.

3.4.1 Functions of Setting

  • Atmosphere: The decaying mansion in "The Fall of the House of Usher" generates Gothic dread before a single plot event occurs.
  • Symbolic reinforcement: The isolated island in Lord of the Flies functions as a controlled "laboratory" setting, removing external social structures so that Golding can isolate his variable of interest — human nature without civilisation's restraints.
  • Social/historical grounding: Lahore in 1998, with the nuclear tests as backdrop, grounds Moth Smoke's class critique in a specific, legible historical moment.
  • Psychological projection: In Poe's fiction, setting frequently externalises a character's interior state — the cracked, decaying Usher mansion mirrors Roderick Usher's disintegrating mind.
Theoretical Note

The concept of the chronotope, developed by Russian theorist Mikhail Bakhtin, describes the intrinsic connectedness of temporal and spatial relationships in literature — the idea that time and space are not separable backdrop elements but are fused into a single artistic whole that shapes the kinds of events and characters a narrative can contain. The island chronotope of Lord of the Flies, for example, is generically linked to the adventure and "desert island" tradition it both invokes and subverts.

3.5 Point of View (POV)

Point of view is the perspective from which a narrative is presented to the reader, determining what information is available, how it is filtered, and with whom the reader is invited to identify. POV is arguably the single most consequential technical choice an author makes, since it controls the reader's epistemic access to the storyworld.

3.5.1 The Major Categories

  • First person: A character-narrator uses "I," offering subjective intimacy but inherently limited and potentially biased knowledge.
  • Second person: The rare use of "you," which can create unusual intimacy or directly implicate the reader, as in certain chapters of Moth Smoke.
  • Third person limited: An external narrator restricts access to a single character's consciousness.
  • Third person omniscient: An external narrator has unrestricted access to all characters' thoughts and feelings, as well as authority to comment on events — the mode Golding uses throughout Lord of the Flies.

3.5.2 The Unreliable Narrator

An unreliable narrator is a narrating voice whose account the reader has reason to distrust, whether due to limited knowledge, psychological instability, self-interest, or deliberate deception. Wayne Booth, in The Rhetoric of Fiction (1961), distinguished such narrators by the degree to which they diverge from the "implied author's" norms — the reader senses a gap between what the narrator claims and what the text, through irony or contradiction, reveals to be true. Daru's self-justifying first-person account in Moth Smoke is the course's central example: his narration consistently minimises his own culpability while emphasising his victimhood, a discrepancy the novel's multi-voiced structure exposes by juxtaposing his account against others'.

🎯 Examiner Tip

Do not confuse "first person" with "unreliable" — they are independent variables. A first-person narrator can be entirely trustworthy, and in rare cases even a third-person narrator can mislead through selective omission. Always justify unreliability with specific textual evidence (contradiction, self-interest, or limited knowledge), not simply because the narrator speaks in "I."

3.6 Theme

Theme is the central insight, concern, or argument a literary work develops about human experience, society, or existence. Theme should be distinguished carefully from both subject matter (what a text is "about" on the surface) and moral (a simple didactic lesson). A theme is typically best articulated as a statement or proposition rather than a single word: not simply "civilisation," but "civilisation is a fragile social construct that can collapse rapidly once external authority is removed, revealing an innate human capacity for savagery."

3.6.1 How Theme Is Constructed

Theme rarely appears as direct authorial statement (with non-fictional essays being a partial exception); rather, it is constructed cumulatively through plot outcomes, character arcs, symbolism, and patterns of imagery. In Lord of the Flies, theme emerges through the symbolic trajectory of the conch shell (intact → cracked → destroyed) tracking the parallel collapse of civilised order.

⭐ Examiner Favourite

Long questions frequently ask students to "discuss the central theme" of a text. Always state the theme as a complete proposition (not a single word), then trace its development through at least three specific textual moments or symbols.

3.7 Style and Tone

Style refers to an author's characteristic and distinctive use of language — diction (word choice), syntax (sentence construction), imagery, and rhetorical devices. Tone refers more specifically to the author's or narrator's attitude toward the subject matter, conveyed through these same stylistic choices.

3.7.1 Distinguishing Tone from Mood

Students frequently conflate tone with mood, but the two are conceptually distinct: tone originates with the author/narrator (their attitude), while mood is the emotional atmosphere experienced by the reader. A text can have an ironic tone (Chopin's narrative stance toward Louise Mallard's "tragedy") that produces a complex, unsettled mood in the reader — simultaneously sympathetic and quietly subversive.

3.7.2 Style as Analytical Evidence

At university level, style should never be discussed in the abstract ("the author's style is good") but always demonstrated through close reading of specific linguistic choices. Compare three contrasting styles studied in this course:

  • Orwell: Plain, concrete, declarative diction; short, direct sentences; a confessional first-person tone that creates an effect of unflinching honesty even while documenting moral failure.
  • Poe: Dense, ornate, Latinate diction; long, syntactically complex sentences with extensive subordination, mirroring the suffocating, claustrophobic atmosphere of his Gothic settings.
  • Hemingway: Sparse, paratactic prose (short independent clauses joined with minimal subordination); deliberate omission of emotional commentary, in line with his Iceberg Theory (Chapter 2).
🎯 Examiner Tip

For style/tone questions, quote or closely paraphrase a brief stylistic feature (sentence length, diction register, a specific rhetorical device) rather than offering vague praise. Examiners reward textual specificity far more than general adjectives like "beautiful" or "powerful."

3.8 Chapter Summary

  • Plot is the causally structured arrangement of events; Freytag's Pyramid (exposition, rising action, climax, falling action, denouement) remains the standard model, though non-linear texts like Moth Smoke require the fabula/syuzhet distinction.
  • Characterization operates through direct and indirect methods; Forster's round/flat distinction and the dynamic/static distinction are essential analytical tools.
  • Setting performs active thematic and psychological work, not merely passive backdrop; Bakhtin's "chronotope" formalises the fusion of time and space in narrative meaning.
  • Point of view governs the reader's epistemic access; the unreliable narrator (Booth) creates a gap between narration and implied truth.
  • Theme is best articulated as a full proposition, constructed cumulatively through plot, symbol, and character rather than stated directly.
  • Style (linguistic choices) and tone (attitude conveyed through those choices) must be distinguished from mood (the reader's emotional response) and demonstrated through specific textual evidence.
