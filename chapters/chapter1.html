1.1 Introduction

Every piece of writing a reader encounters falls, broadly, into one of two great families of narrative: the fictional and the non-fictional. This distinction is not a modern invention — it stretches back to Aristotle's Poetics, where he separated history (which tells "what has happened") from poetry, or imaginative literature (which tells "what may happen," that is, what is possible according to probability or necessity). Aristotle's insight remains the seed of the entire distinction we study in this chapter: history and non-fiction are bound to the actual; fiction is bound to the possible.

Understanding this distinction is the single most important theoretical foundation of ENG-201. Almost every other concept in this course — point of view, characterization, theme, the forms of the novel, the forms of the essay — depends on first understanding whether a text is operating within the fictional mode or the non-fictional mode, because the two modes carry different obligations to the reader, different relationships to truth, and different aesthetic strategies.

This chapter develops a precise, examinable definition of both categories, lays out the criteria by which texts are sorted into one or the other, and — crucially for a university-level treatment — interrogates the limits of the distinction itself. As we shall see in Section 1.5, the boundary between fiction and non-fiction is not as stable as introductory treatments suggest, and a sophisticated answer in examination should always acknowledge this nuance.

1.2 Fictional Narrative: A Formal Definition

A fictional narrative is a prose composition in which the events, characters, and (frequently) the setting are products of the author's imagination. The defining criterion is not that fiction contains no truth — indeed, fiction often communicates psychological, moral, or social truths more powerfully than factual writing — but that its propositional content is not subject to a truth-claim in the way a newspaper report or a historical chronicle is. When Golding writes that a group of boys descend into savagery on an island, he is not asserting that this event occurred; he is constructing an imagined scenario through which a thesis about human nature can be explored.

1.2.1 Core Characteristics of Fiction

  • Invented content: Events, characters, and often settings are created rather than reported, even when inspired by real experience (a process literary theorists call "transmutation" of lived material into art).
  • Suspension of disbelief: Coleridge's famous phrase describes the reader's willing acceptance of the fictional premise in exchange for imaginative and emotional engagement.
  • Mimetic and didactic potential: Fiction can imitate (mimesis) lived experience while also instructing or revealing (didaxis) — Golding's allegory is simultaneously a gripping adventure narrative and a philosophical argument about innate evil.
  • Authorial freedom: The author bears no obligation to factual accuracy, though internal consistency (a coherent "storyworld") is still expected.
  • Aesthetic shaping: Events are selected, compressed, and arranged for artistic effect — what Aristotle called plot (mythos) as opposed to mere chronicle.

1.2.2 The Forms Fiction Takes

At the prose level — the level relevant to this course — fiction principally takes three forms, distinguished primarily by length and the corresponding scope of development they allow: the novel, the novella, and the short story. These are treated in full depth in Chapter 2. For now, note simply that length is not a trivial or merely quantitative distinction — it determines what kind of plot, characterization, and thematic complexity a text can sustain. A short story cannot develop a protagonist across decades the way a novel like Moth Smoke can; it must instead achieve what Poe called "unity of effect," a single concentrated impression achieved through economy.

⭐ Examiner Favourite

"Define fictional narrative and explain its primary purpose" is one of the most consistently repeated short questions across FBISE, Punjab Board, and affiliated BISE papers. A complete answer must include: (1) the definition centred on invented content, (2) at least one named theorist or concept (Aristotle's mimesis, Coleridge's suspension of disbelief), and (3) the named forms (novel, novella, short story).

1.3 Non-fictional Narrative: A Formal Definition

A non-fictional narrative is a prose composition that makes a truth-claim about real people, events, or ideas, and for which the author bears responsibility for factual accuracy. Non-fiction does not preclude artistry — indeed, the personal essay and the literary memoir are among the most stylistically refined forms in the English tradition — but it operates under what literary theorist Philippe Lejeune termed an "autobiographical pact" (or, more broadly, a referential pact): an implicit contract with the reader that what is described corresponds to an external reality.

1.3.1 Core Characteristics of Non-fiction

  • Referentiality: Statements can, in principle, be checked against external reality, documentary evidence, or testimony.
  • Authorial accountability: The writer is answerable for accuracy; deliberate fabrication presented as fact constitutes a breach of the genre's contract (and, in journalism, an ethical violation).
  • Variable subjectivity: Non-fiction is not synonymous with objectivity. The personal essay (e.g. Orwell's "Shooting an Elephant") is deeply subjective in voice while remaining non-fictional in its referential claim.
  • Reflective and argumentative purpose: Much non-fiction exists to inform, persuade, record, or reflect — purposes distinct from, though not always exclusive of, fiction's exploratory and aesthetic aims.

1.3.2 The Forms Non-fiction Takes

This course identifies five principal forms of non-fictional narrative: the essay (in its personal, critical, and nature sub-types), biography and autobiography, the travelogue, the memoir, and creative non-fiction. These are explored fully in Chapter 5. Each form negotiates the balance between factual obligation and literary technique differently — the memoir, for instance, permits far more selective and impressionistic treatment of memory than a formal biography would.

🎯 Examiner Tip

Do not define non-fiction as "writing without style" or "boring factual writing." University-level answers should explicitly state that non-fiction can be highly stylised and subjective (cite Orwell or Sontag) while still being non-fictional, because the defining criterion is the truth-claim, not the absence of craft.

Theoretical Note

Aristotle's distinction in the Poetics remains foundational: history narrates "what has happened," a record of the particular and contingent, while poetry (his term for imaginative literature) narrates "what may happen" according to probability or necessity — the universal. This is why Aristotle considered poetry "more philosophical" than history: fiction, freed from the obligation to report the merely actual, can construct situations that reveal general truths about human character and choice.

1.5 The Blurred Boundary: Creative Non-fiction and Autofiction

A genuinely university-level treatment of this topic cannot stop at a clean binary. In contemporary literary practice, the line between fiction and non-fiction is frequently and deliberately destabilised. Two developments are particularly important for ENG-201 students to understand:

1.5.1 Creative Non-fiction

Creative non-fiction applies literary techniques — scene-setting, dialogue reconstruction, figurative language, narrative pacing — to factual material. Susan Sontag's Illness as Metaphor, studied in Chapter 8, is an instructive example: it is rigorously argumentative and grounded in real medical and cultural history, yet its prose deploys rhetorical and stylistic devices more commonly associated with literary fiction. The genre asks readers to accept literary shaping of fact without surrendering the underlying truth-claim.

1.5.2 Autofiction

Autofiction (a term coined by French novelist Serge Doubrovsky in 1977) names texts that blend autobiographical material with fictional invention, often using the author's own name for a protagonist whose experiences nonetheless diverge from documented fact. Such texts deliberately frustrate Lejeune's "autobiographical pact," forcing readers to hold both registers — fiction and non-fiction — simultaneously.

1.5.3 Implications for This Course

Even the novels studied in this course are not free of this tension. Mohsin Hamid's Moth Smoke (Chapter 6), though formally a novel, is set against precisely dated real historical events — Pakistan's nuclear tests of May 1998 — and depicts social and economic conditions in Lahore with documentary specificity. This grounding in verifiable history does not make the novel non-fictional (Daru, Ozi, and Mumtaz remain invented characters), but it illustrates how fictional narrative routinely borrows the authority and texture of the real. A strong answer in long-question format should be able to discuss this kind of border case.

🎯 Examiner Tip

For 10-mark questions asking you to "discuss the distinction" or "evaluate whether the fiction/non-fiction binary is absolute," always devote your final paragraph to this blurred boundary. Examiners at the university level specifically reward students who move beyond a simple two-column definition and demonstrate awareness of contested or hybrid cases.

1.6 Why This Distinction Matters for Literary Study

Beyond the examination hall, this distinction governs how we read. When we open a non-fictional essay such as Mill's "On Liberty," we evaluate its claims as we would any argument — for logical coherence, evidentiary support, and persuasive force. When we open a fictional novel such as Lord of the Flies, we do not ask "did this literally happen?" but rather "what does this invented scenario reveal about civilisation, power, and human nature?" The interpretive questions we bring to a text are conditioned by which side of the fiction/non-fiction divide it occupies.

This is also why genre misclassification is a serious critical error. Reading a memoir as though it were straightforward, unmediated fact ignores the selective and constructed nature of memory; reading a postmodern novel like Moth Smoke as though its multiple narrators were simply unreliable witnesses to be cross-examined for "the truth" misses the novel's deliberate point — that in a corrupt social system, truth itself becomes a contested, class-inflected commodity.

1.7 Chapter Summary

  • Fictional narrative is defined by invented content and the absence of a truth-claim; its purpose is aesthetic, exploratory, and often symbolically truthful despite being factually invented.
  • Non-fictional narrative is defined by a referential truth-claim and authorial accountability to fact; it can nonetheless be highly stylised and subjective.
  • Aristotle's Poetics provides the foundational theoretical distinction: history (the particular, the actual) versus poetry (the universal, the possible).
  • Coleridge's "suspension of disbelief" and Lejeune's "autobiographical/referential pact" describe the differing contracts each mode makes with the reader.
  • The boundary is not absolute: creative non-fiction and autofiction deliberately blend the two modes, and even canonical novels (e.g. Moth Smoke) borrow heavily from documented historical reality.
  • This foundational distinction shapes how every subsequent concept in ENG-201 — POV, characterization, theme, genre — should be analysed.
