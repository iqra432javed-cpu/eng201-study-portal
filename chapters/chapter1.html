Section I

Theory of Narrative

This section covers the foundational theoretical concepts required for ENG-201. Examiners consistently draw short and long questions from these definitions and distinctions.

1. Fictional vs Non-fictional Narrative

⭐ Examiner Favourite — Define & Distinguish

The most frequently asked question in ENG-201 exams. Know both definitions precisely and have at least two distinguishing points.

Fictional Narrative

A fictional narrative is an imaginative account of events, characters, and settings created by the author's imagination. Although it may draw on real experiences, the core content is invented. Fiction aims to entertain, explore human experience, and convey deeper truths through invented stories.

  • Events are imagined, not factually verified
  • Characters are created (though may be based on real people)
  • Primary purpose: artistic expression, entertainment, exploration of human condition
  • Forms: Novel, Novella, Short Story

Non-fictional Narrative

A non-fictional narrative deals with real events, real people, and factual information. The author writes about actual occurrences and verifiable truths, though style and structure may vary greatly.

  • Events are real and verifiable
  • Author is responsible for factual accuracy
  • Primary purpose: inform, argue, record, reflect
  • Forms: Essay, Biography, Autobiography, Travelogue, Memoir, Creative Non-fiction
🎯 Examiner Tip

Always state that the distinction is not always absolute — creative non-fiction and autofiction blur the boundary. Examiners reward nuanced answers.

2. Key Forms of Fictional Prose Narrative

The Novel

The novel is the most prominent form of long prose fiction. It is typically over 40,000 words and features complex plots, developed characters, and sustained exploration of theme.

  • Originated in the 18th century (Defoe, Richardson, Fielding)
  • Allows for psychological depth, multiple storylines, and social commentary
  • Examples in course: Lord of the Flies (Golding), Moth Smoke (Hamid)

The Short Story

The short story is a brief prose fiction, typically between 1,000 and 20,000 words, focused on a single event, character, or moment. Unlike the novel, it demands compression and precision.

  • Single dominant effect (Poe's theory of "unity of effect")
  • Limited characters, compressed time, concentrated theme
  • Often ends with a twist or epiphany
  • Examples: "Story of an Hour," "The Necklace," "Araby," "The Fall of the House of Usher"

The Novella

The novella is intermediate in length (15,000–40,000 words) between the short story and novel. It has the compression of a short story but allows for deeper development than the short story permits.

  • Examples: Heart of Darkness (Conrad), The Metamorphosis (Kafka)

3. Key Concepts / Elements of Fictional Prose

⭐ High-Yield — All Elements May Be Asked

Know definitions of all six elements with examples from prescribed texts.

Plot Structure

Plot is the sequence of events in a narrative. Freytag's Pyramid outlines: Exposition → Rising Action → Climax → Falling Action → Denouement (Resolution).

  • Exposition: Background and introduction of characters/setting
  • Rising Action: Conflict develops; tension builds
  • Climax: Turning point — highest tension
  • Falling Action: Consequences of climax unfold
  • Denouement: Resolution — conflicts resolved (or not)

Characters and Characterization

Characterization is the method by which an author creates and develops characters. Two main methods:

  • Direct/Explicit: Author tells us directly about the character
  • Indirect/Dramatic: Character revealed through actions, speech, thoughts, others' reactions
  • Round characters: Complex, multi-dimensional (e.g., Daru in Moth Smoke)
  • Flat characters: One-dimensional, type characters
  • Dynamic: Change through the story; Static: Remain unchanged

Setting

Setting refers to the time, place, and social environment of a narrative. It functions to create atmosphere, establish context, and reflect character psychology.

Point of View (POV)

Point of View is the narrative perspective from which the story is told.

  • First Person: Narrator is a character ("I") — subjective, limited knowledge. E.g., Moth Smoke
  • Second Person: "You" — rare, creates intimacy or implication
  • Third Person Limited: Outside narrator focuses on one character's mind
  • Third Person Omniscient: All-knowing narrator has access to all characters' thoughts and feelings. E.g., Lord of the Flies
  • Unreliable Narrator: A narrator whose credibility is compromised — creates dramatic irony
🎯 Examiner Tip

In Moth Smoke, multiple first-person narrators create an unreliable, fragmented perspective — always mention this in POV questions.

Theme

Theme is the central idea or underlying message of a literary work. It is not a moral but an insight about life, human nature, or society.

  • Lord of the Flies: Civilisation vs. Savagery; Loss of Innocence; Power
  • Moth Smoke: Class inequality; Moral decay; Pakistan's identity crisis
  • "Story of an Hour": Freedom; Oppression; Marriage; Death as liberation

Style and Tone

Style is the author's distinctive way of writing — including diction, sentence structure, imagery, and use of literary devices.

Tone is the author's attitude toward the subject matter, conveyed through language choices. It differs from mood (the emotional atmosphere felt by the reader).

  • Orwell: Plain, direct, ironic tone
  • Poe: Gothic, atmospheric, tense tone
  • Chopin: Ironic, restrained, subversive tone

4. The Novel and Its Genres

⭐ Examiner Favourite — Genre Definitions

Short questions on individual genres appear in nearly every paper. Memorise key features + one example for each.

Epistolary Novel

Told entirely through letters, diary entries, or documents. Gives a sense of immediacy and authenticity. Example: Pamela (Richardson), Frankenstein (Shelley)

The Picaresque Novel

Follows a roguish hero (picaro) on episodic adventures across social classes. Satirises society. Origin: Spanish tradition. Example: Tom Jones (Fielding)

Coming-of-Age / Bildungsroman

Traces the psychological and moral growth of a protagonist from youth to maturity. Bildungsroman = German for "novel of formation." Example: Great Expectations (Dickens), Jane Eyre (Brontë)

The Historical Novel

Set in a specific historical period; recreates past events and social conditions with fictional characters. Example: A Tale of Two Cities (Dickens)

The Novel of Manners

Focuses on the customs, values, and behaviours of a particular social class. Often satirical. Example: Pride and Prejudice (Austen)

The Gothic Novel

Combines horror, mystery, and supernatural elements with psychological terror. Dark settings, brooding atmosphere. Example: The Fall of the House of Usher (Poe) — also a Gothic short story.

The Realistic Novel

Represents ordinary life and society truthfully, without idealisation or romanticism. Example: Middlemarch (Eliot)

Modern Novel

Post-1900 fiction that experiments with narrative structure, stream of consciousness, and fragmented time. Example: Mrs Dalloway (Woolf), Ulysses (Joyce)

Postmodern Novel

Self-referential, metafictional, challenges the idea of a stable narrative or truth. Uses irony, intertextuality, and fragmentation. Example: Moth Smoke (Hamid), Slaughterhouse-Five (Vonnegut)

Graphic Novel

A narrative told through sequential art/comics, treated as serious literary fiction. Example: Maus (Spiegelman), Persepolis (Satrapi)

Other Forms

Detective, Mystery, Thriller, Fantasy, Dystopian Fiction, Science Fiction — each defined by genre conventions and reader expectations.

🎯 Examiner Tip

Moth Smoke can be discussed as a postmodern, multi-voiced novel with noir detective elements — use this multi-genre reading in long answers for full marks.

5. Key Forms of Non-fictional Narrative

The Essay

A short piece of non-fiction that explores a topic from the writer's personal perspective. Three types required:

  • Personal Essay: Reflective, first-person, informal — explores the author's own experiences (e.g., Orwell)
  • Critical Essay: Analytical, evaluates a work of art or literature
  • Nature Essay: Focuses on natural world, often combines observation with reflection

Biography & Autobiography

Biography: A factual account of a person's life written by someone else.
Autobiography: The author writes about their own life — a first-person narrative of self.

Travelogue

A narrative account of a journey. Combines observation, personal reflection, cultural commentary, and descriptive writing.

Memoir

A first-person account focused on a specific period or theme from the author's life — more selective and thematic than full autobiography.

Creative Non-fiction

Uses literary techniques (imagery, metaphor, narrative structure) in factual writing. Blurs the boundary between fiction and non-fiction.

Section II

Theory into Action — Prescribed Texts

Section II applies theoretical concepts to close reading of selected texts. Below are essential notes on all prescribed texts.

6. Novels

Lord of the Flies — William Golding (1954)

⭐ Most Important Novel — Always in Exam

Summary: A group of British schoolboys are stranded on an uninhabited island after their plane is shot down. They attempt to govern themselves but descend into savagery and violence.

Key Characters:

  • Ralph: Protagonist; represents civilisation, democracy, order
  • Jack: Antagonist; represents savagery, desire for power, instinct
  • Piggy: Reason, intellect, scientific thinking; marginalised
  • Simon: Spiritual insight, natural goodness; Christ-like figure
  • Roger: Pure sadism; cruelty without restraint
  • The Beast: Symbol of innate human evil and fear

Themes: Civilisation vs. Savagery · Loss of Innocence · Power & Democracy vs. Tyranny · Human Evil · The Failure of Reason

Symbolism:

  • Conch shell: Democratic order, legitimate authority
  • The Beast: Innate human evil (Simon's revelation: "the beast is us")
  • Piggy's glasses: Reason and intellectual vision
  • Fire: Civilisation and hope (or destruction)
  • Lord of the Flies (pig's head): Beelzebub / Devil — pure evil

POV: Third person omniscient — Golding controls irony and the reader's moral judgement.

🎯 Examiner Tip

Golding said he wrote the novel as a response to Coral Island by R.M. Ballantyne, which depicted boys as naturally good. Always mention this allegorical context for full marks.

Moth Smoke — Mohsin Hamid (2000)

Summary: Daru Shezad, a banker in Lahore who loses his job, descends into drugs, poverty, and criminality while conducting an affair with his best friend Ozi's wife, Mumtaz. Set against 1998 Pakistan's nuclear tests and elite corruption.

Key Characters:

  • Daru: Protagonist; moral ambiguity, self-destruction
  • Ozi: The privileged elite; corruption through immunity
  • Mumtaz: Trapped between two worlds; agency and desire
  • Murad Badshah: Entrepreneur of crime; social commentary

Themes: Class inequality · Moral decay · Postcolonial identity · Love & Betrayal · Pakistan's nuclear-age identity crisis

Structure: Multi-voiced, postmodern — different narrators give conflicting testimonies (Daru's trial frames the novel). Unreliable narration throughout.

Title Symbolism: The moth drawn to the flame of the elite lifestyle is destroyed by it — Daru is the moth; the world of wealth is the lethal flame.

7. Short Stories

"Story of an Hour" — Kate Chopin (1894)

Plot: Louise Mallard, a woman with a heart condition, is told her husband Brently has died in a train accident. Alone in her room, she experiences not grief but a profound sense of freedom. When Brently walks through the door unharmed, she dies — the doctor says of "joy that kills," but the irony suggests it is the death of her newly discovered freedom.

Themes: Freedom & Oppression · The Institution of Marriage · Female Independence · Irony of Death

Key Technique: Dramatic irony — the reader understands Louise's joy is liberation, not grief; society misreads it entirely.

🎯 Examiner Tip

Always discuss Chopin as a proto-feminist writer and the social context of 1890s America — this earns extra marks in analysis questions.

"The Fall of the House of Usher" — Edgar Allan Poe (1839)

Plot: The unnamed narrator visits his childhood friend Roderick Usher at his crumbling mansion. Roderick's twin sister Madeline, who suffers from catalepsy, is entombed alive. She returns from the vault and falls on Roderick, both dying together as the house collapses.

Themes: Gothic horror · Psychological deterioration · Death & Decay · Incest (implied) · The supernatural vs. psychological

Gothic Elements: Decaying mansion, mysterious illness, premature burial, atmospheric dread, unreliable narrator, supernatural coincidence.

Symbolism: The house = Roderick's mind (both crack and collapse simultaneously).

"The Necklace" — Guy de Maupassant (1884)

Plot: Mathilde Loisel, a middle-class woman obsessed with wealth, borrows a diamond necklace for a ball, loses it, and spends 10 years in poverty to replace it — only to discover the original was fake.

Themes: Vanity & Pride · Social class · Fate & Irony · The cost of deception

Key Technique: Situational irony — the twist at the end reframes the entire story.

"Cat in the Rain" — Ernest Hemingway (1925)

Plot: An American couple in an Italian hotel. The wife sees a cat sheltering from the rain and wants to rescue it; her husband remains indifferent. A hotel owner sends her a cat — possibly not the same one.

Themes: Marital alienation · Female desire for nurturing and identity · Isolation in a foreign place

Iceberg Theory: Hemingway never states the wife's dissatisfaction directly — the cat symbolises her unmet emotional needs, desire for home, and feminine identity.

"Araby" — James Joyce (1914)

Plot: A young Dublin boy becomes infatuated with his friend's sister. He promises to bring her something from the Araby bazaar. He arrives late; the bazaar is almost closed and cheap. He experiences a moment of devastating self-realisation.

Themes: Loss of innocence · Disillusionment · The gap between romantic fantasy and reality · Colonised Dublin

Epiphany: Joyce's concept — a sudden moment of spiritual or psychological revelation. The boy's epiphany is recognising his own vanity and the falseness of his romantic idealisation.

8. Essays

"Of Marriage and Single Life" — Francis Bacon (c.1612)

Type: Formal/Philosophical Essay. Bacon weighs the advantages and disadvantages of married vs single life for men of public affairs.

Key Ideas: Married men are better citizens but less free for great enterprise; single men are better friends and servants; the wife and children become "hostages to fortune."

Style: Aphoristic, dense, Latin-influenced. Short, epigrammatic sentences. No personal revelation — purely argumentative.

"A Free Man's Worship" — Bertrand Russell (1903)

Type: Philosophical Essay. Russell argues that in a universe indifferent to human values (materialist worldview), human beings must create their own meaning through the "worship" of goodness, beauty, and truth.

Key Ideas: Universe has no inherent purpose; human dignity comes from refusing to worship power; freedom lies in embracing what is genuinely good despite knowing it is temporary.

"Shooting an Elephant" — George Orwell (1936)

⭐ Most Important Essay — Frequently Examined

Type: Personal/Political Essay. Orwell, a British police officer in Burma, is pressured by the local crowd to shoot a working elephant that has gone rogue. He shoots it despite knowing it is wrong — to avoid appearing weak before the colonised population.

Themes: Imperialism · Loss of moral agency · The psychology of power · Hypocrisy of colonialism · The oppressor also oppressed

Key Argument: The colonial officer becomes a puppet of the very people he oppresses — he must act against his conscience to maintain the illusion of authority. "When the white man turns tyrant it is his own freedom that he destroys."

Style: Plain, direct, vivid, deeply self-critical. First-person confession mode.

"On Liberty" (Chapter 1) — John Stuart Mill (1859)

Type: Political Philosophy Essay. Mill argues for individual liberty against social tyranny. The harm principle: the only justification for restricting individual freedom is to prevent harm to others.

Key Ideas: Tyranny of the majority is as dangerous as tyranny of government; freedom of thought and expression are essential; individuality must be protected.

"Illness as Metaphor" (Chapter 9) — Susan Sontag (1978)

Type: Critical/Cultural Essay. Sontag argues that society uses illness (particularly TB and cancer) as metaphors that stigmatise patients and distort thinking about disease.

Key Argument: Illness should be seen as a purely physical phenomenon, not a reflection of character or moral failure. Metaphors of "battle," "invasion," and "defeat" are harmful to patients.

🎯 Examiner Tip

Sontag's essay is unique — it critiques how language shapes perception of illness. Link her argument to structuralism/post-structuralism if possible for a high-scoring answer.
