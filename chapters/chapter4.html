4.1 Introduction

Since its emergence in the eighteenth century, the novel has differentiated into a wide range of genres, each defined by characteristic narrative strategies, thematic preoccupations, and historical contexts. Understanding genre is not a matter of mechanical classification — genres carry inherited conventions and reader expectations that authors can fulfil, subvert, or hybridise for artistic effect. This chapter surveys ten genre traditions specified in the ENG-201 syllabus, tracing each from its historical origin to its relevance for the novels studied in Chapter 6.

Mindmap — Genres of the Novel

4.2 The Ten Genres

4.2.1 The Epistolary Novel

A novel composed entirely (or substantially) of letters, diary entries, or other documents. The form, pioneered in English by Samuel Richardson's Pamela (1740), creates an effect of immediacy and psychological intimacy, since the reader receives events as the characters experience and record them, without an intervening narrator. Mary Shelley's Frankenstein (1818) uses nested epistolary frames (Walton's letters containing Frankenstein's narration containing the Creature's account) to dramatise questions of reliability and perspective.

4.2.2 The Picaresque Novel

Originating in 16th-century Spain with the anonymous Lazarillo de Tormes (1554), the picaresque follows a roguish, low-born protagonist (the picaro) through a loosely connected sequence of episodic adventures, typically across multiple social strata, allowing the genre to function as a vehicle for social satire. Henry Fielding's Tom Jones (1749) is the canonical English example.

4.2.3 The Bildungsroman (Coming-of-Age Novel)

German for "novel of formation," the Bildungsroman traces a protagonist's psychological, moral, and social development from childhood or youth toward maturity, typically structured around a series of formative trials. Charles Dickens's Great Expectations (1861) and Charlotte Brontë's Jane Eyre (1847) are paradigmatic examples in the English tradition. Note that Lord of the Flies, while not a conventional Bildungsroman, inverts its expectations — the boys' island experience produces moral regression rather than maturation, a deliberately ironic engagement with the genre's usual arc of growth.

4.2.4 The Historical Novel

Set in a clearly identified past period, the historical novel combines invented characters and plot with documented historical events, conditions, or figures. Walter Scott is widely credited with establishing the modern form with Waverley (1814); Dickens's A Tale of Two Cities (1859), set during the French Revolution, is a further canonical example. The genre requires careful negotiation between historical fidelity and the freedoms of fictional invention discussed in Chapter 1.

4.2.5 The Novel of Manners

Concerned primarily with the customs, values, and codes of conduct of a particular social class, the novel of manners (most associated with Jane Austen) typically deploys irony and social satire to expose hypocrisy or rigidity within its depicted milieu. Pride and Prejudice (1813) remains the genre's most frequently cited exemplar.

4.2.6 The Gothic Novel

Emerging with Horace Walpole's The Castle of Otranto (1764), the Gothic novel combines elements of horror, mystery, and the supernatural with intense psychological atmosphere, typically set within decaying or claustrophobic architectural spaces (castles, mansions, crypts). Edgar Allan Poe's short fiction, including "The Fall of the House of Usher" (Chapter 7), represents the genre's distillation into compact prose form, substituting architectural and psychological collapse for the longer Gothic novel's plot of inheritance and concealed crime.

4.2.7 The Realistic Novel

A reaction against Romantic idealisation, literary realism (flourishing especially in the mid-to-late 19th century) seeks to represent ordinary life, characters, and social conditions with documentary accuracy and psychological plausibility, avoiding melodrama or implausible coincidence. George Eliot's Middlemarch (1871–72) exemplifies the form's commitment to richly detailed social and moral observation.

4.2.8 The Modern Novel

The modernist novel (roughly 1900–1945) experiments radically with narrative form — stream of consciousness, fragmented chronology, multiple unreliable perspectives — reflecting broader modernist concerns with subjectivity, the instability of meaning, and the inadequacy of conventional realist representation after the upheavals of the early twentieth century. Virginia Woolf's Mrs Dalloway (1925) and James Joyce's Ulysses (1922) are foundational texts; Joyce's short fiction in Dubliners (studied in Chapter 7) anticipates these techniques in miniature.

4.2.9 The Postmodern Novel

Postmodern fiction extends modernist formal experimentation while adding a self-conscious, often ironic awareness of its own constructedness (metafiction), frequently incorporating fragmented or contradictory narrators, genre-blending, and explicit skepticism toward unified or authoritative "truth." Mohsin Hamid's Moth Smoke (Chapter 6) is the course's primary postmodern text: its multi-voiced, trial-framed structure denies readers a single authoritative account, while its noir/detective genre borrowings (a crime, conflicting testimonies, an implicated reader-as-judge) exemplify the postmodern strategy of genre hybridity discussed below.

4.2.10 The Graphic Novel

A long-form narrative told through sequential art combined with text, the graphic novel has, since the late twentieth century, achieved recognition as a serious literary form capable of the same thematic depth as prose fiction. Art Spiegelman's Maus (1980–91), a Holocaust narrative using anthropomorphic animals, and Marjane Satrapi's Persepolis (2000–03), an autobiographical account of growing up during the Iranian Revolution, demonstrate the form's capacity for historical and political seriousness.

Other Recognised Forms

Beyond the ten genres detailed above, the syllabus also notes several popular and commercially significant genre categories — detective fiction, mystery, thriller, fantasy, dystopian fiction, and science fiction — each governed by distinct genre conventions and reader expectations, though these receive less sustained theoretical attention in the literary tradition this course emphasises.

4.3 Genre Hybridity: A Critical Perspective

A genuinely advanced answer on novel genres should resist treating these categories as mutually exclusive boxes. Most significant novels — including both texts studied in Chapter 6 — draw on multiple genre traditions simultaneously, and recognising this hybridity is itself a marker of sophisticated literary analysis.

Moth Smoke, for instance, can be productively read as simultaneously: a postmodern novel (fragmented, multi-voiced, metafictionally aware of its own trial-framing); a work with noir/detective elements (a crime, conflicting witness testimony, moral ambiguity about guilt); and, more loosely, a novel of manners insofar as it satirises the customs and hypocrisies of Lahore's wealthy elite. Lord of the Flies, while most readily classified within the allegorical/dystopian tradition, also self-consciously engages and inverts the conventions of the boys'-adventure and Bildungsroman traditions it was written to critique (recall its origin as a deliberate response to Ballantyne's The Coral Island, discussed further in Chapter 6).

🎯 Examiner Tip

For 10-mark questions asking students to "discuss the major genres of the novel," the strongest answers conclude with a paragraph on hybridity, explicitly arguing that a single text (ideally Moth Smoke) productively combines two or more of the genres discussed. This demonstrates synthesis rather than rote listing.

4.4 Chapter Summary

  • Ten genre traditions are central to this course: epistolary, picaresque, Bildungsroman, historical, novel of manners, Gothic, realistic, modern, postmodern, and graphic novel.
  • Each genre carries a distinct historical origin and a set of formal conventions that shape reader expectation.
  • The Bildungsroman tradition is especially relevant to Lord of the Flies, which inverts its conventional arc of maturation into one of moral regression.
  • The postmodern genre, exemplified by Moth Smoke, is defined by fragmentation, multiple unreliable narrators, and metafictional self-awareness.
  • Sophisticated literary analysis recognises genre hybridity — most significant novels, including both texts studied in this course, combine multiple genre traditions rather than belonging to a single fixed category.
